What is the Course About?

Our course, Design and Analysis of Algorithms, introduces us to the fundamentals of algorithms and data structures, focusing on their design, analysis, and application. We start with core concepts like time and space complexities, recursion, and backtracking. Next, we delve into tools like hashing and Union-Find, along with advanced data structures such as AVL Trees, Segment Trees, and Red-Black Trees. Sorting and searching algorithms, including Quick Sort, KMP, and Rabin-Karp, help us understand efficient data processing techniques. Graph algorithms like Dijkstra’s shortest path and Prim’s spanning tree, and case studies such as the Travelling Salesperson Problem, show us how algorithms solve complex, real-world problems. The course also introduces concepts like NP-completeness and the limitations of algorithmic power. By the end, we gain a strong foundation in computational thinking and the ability to develop efficient solutions to challenging problems.

What kind of data structures and algorithms have we studied so far?

Throughout this course, we have encountered various data structures and algorithms essential for solving complex problems efficiently. Here’s a summary of what we have studied:

  • Math Basics: We began by understanding the fundamental concepts of mathematics that form the foundation for algorithmic analysis, such as time and space complexity.
  • Data Structures: We studied structured data types like binary trees, AVL trees, Red-Black trees, heaps, and graphs, each essential for different problem-solving approaches.
  • Sorting Algorithms: Bubble Sort, Selection Sort, Merge Sort, Quick Sort, and Heap Sort were explored to understand various sorting techniques and their complexities.
  • Graph Algorithms: We also studied graph traversal algorithms like Depth-First Search (DFS), Breadth-First Search (BFS), Dijkstra’s shortest path, and minimum spanning tree algorithms.
  • Advanced Algorithms: We examined more advanced topics like NP-completeness, dynamic programming, greedy algorithms, and backtracking.
